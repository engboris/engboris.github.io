Questions (currently 11 available)

December 5, 2018
constantly updated

Here are some questions I’m interested in. These questions may have no meaning or no true content (Sometimes I’m not even sure myself about the meaning of all that). Maybe there’re already answers or related works. I’m not aware of anything and will update this page according to my current understanding.

  1. How does logic and computation manipulate and is affected by space and time? Linear Logic seems to take logic from mental references to the primitive intuition of space and time. Mathematical proofs which were seen as sequential mental acts evolving through time became proof-nets describing topological manipulations operated by logic. The lost of time is recovered in the works on polarization and focalization, exhibiting a mechanism of interaction by a dialogue between positives and negatives. How does interaction and information evolve in space and time? What can Logic teach us about the study of time (sequentiality and concurrency)? What can the study of space (graph theory, topology) teach us about the mechanisms of logic?

  2. Are reductionist approaches to logic limited? Should we prefer a holistic one? Is exploring the (compositional?) structure of Logic and Computation enough to understand them? Category Theory capture quite well logical and computational phenomenon but one can wonder if something does escape the categorical view. What are the limits of the categorical view? It seems that Category Theory makes some assumptions reflecting how we understand abstract concepts. Categorical entities are high-level and considered up-to-isomorphism thus forgetting location and altering identification (existing in computer’s programs and architectures). While the categorical interpretation of logic identify proofs with the same dynamics (w.r.t cut-elimination) the geometry of interaction relate them with a procedure of execution. Does the categorical view format (positively? negatively?) our understanding?

  3. Does the pragmatic approach of computation solve the identity crisis of Logic? It was once thought that classical logic didn’t have any computational interpretation. Then came a quite surprising interpretation given by the logical interpretation of innocent instructions (such as call/cc from Scheme). Can everyday programming provide a significant creativity to Logic? What is the logical interpretation of usual entities of practical programming? Can the practical use of computer science and programming (which is pragmatic) change our understanding of Logic (always influenced by paths of ideas and our understanding of concepts i.e ontologically subjectively engaged)? It may be interesting to investigate the computational meaning of some great mathematical axioms/theorems.

  4. How are Logic and Computation related to Cognition and Nature? Linear Logic is used in type systems for quantum functional programming languages but also to describe higher-order quantum circuits through proof nets. How is it connected to quantum computation and quantum information led by the laws of quantum mechanics? Is there really any connection at all? Is it an exaggeration to relate cognition, computation, nature, quantum, and logic? Logic and computation seem to be part of the nature but the nature itself doesn’t seem to truly compute or reason in a discrete framework (or maybe even not at all). More directly, are logic and computation part of the nature (i.e does the nature use it as well) or are they a purely artificial exploitation of its potential (e.g logic used as a purely subjective theory of normativity, computation as (artificial/superficial) automation).

  5. What is the origin of historical prejudices and misconceptions of Logic? What does the history of Logic teach us about the way humans think and what/why do they believe? Like Physics and Biology, should Logic be taken as a science (observing interaction as a natural phenomenon)? It may be interesting to investigate the biological, psychological and sociological aspects of Logic. Why was Logic thought and organized the way it was? How can the understanding of our failure prevent us from going in wrong directions?

  6. What makes something difficult or easy? Implicit Computation Complexity allows to express computational complexity classes by syntactical restrictions on formal languages. What makes the complexity of a class seems now to have a shape. It is then interesting to explore the deep structure of these restrictions. It seems that algebraic tools may give an understanding we didn’t have with the algorithmic view. The recent Geometric Complexity Theory seems to provide satisfying answers based on algebraic and geometrical arguments. Moreover, complexity seems to be associated with the formulation of exponentials in Linear Logic and an associated choice of algebra.

  7. What Logic and Computation really are and how are they similar or different? Even today it is not clear what computation and logic are even though we thought we understood several times. The Curry-Howard isomorphism tells us that logic and computation share a common structure and are deeply related. Martin-Löf’s Type Theory and proof assistants exploit the idea by unifying proofs and formulas with programs and types in the same framework. Taking the idea to the extreme, is Logic a mere alias of Convergent Computation? It seems that the answer is no. Something similar to what separate analytic and synthetic seem to deeply separate computation and what we call logic. Logic has both a restrictive (type systems à la Curry) and normative (type systems à la Church) effect on computational behaviors (untyped computation) which can be both formulated within Proof Theory thanks to the cut-elimination procedure. What about the purely locative and interactional Turing Machines? Where do they stand?

  8. What is the common ground of syntactical formats? Some formalisms took a particular format due to (mainly) historical reasons but are equivalent to other ones. For instance, inductive description of regular languages, finite state automata, regular grammars, regular expressions and read-only Turing machines all define regular languages. How can they be abstracted to only keep interactional potential and use over information/data? Although similar, equivalent formats are far from being strictly/really equivalent. How do they differ? Some concepts seems to be heavily obturated by heavy bureaucracy (Category Theory?). How can we produce a more fertile and simple syntax to describe a concept? In a more administrative aspect of mathematical practice, can we provide a general study of efficient use of syntactical formats? How do concepts become syntax? Is a study of the administrative aspects of mathematical practice and formats possible? More generally, it is about a reflection on the compilation from concepts (specification) to syntax (usable/sharable entities). The BHK interpretation is such a concept involving several syntaxes.

  9. What is non-determinism and what is its role in the human/machine duality? It is about “true” non-determinism. A kind of non-determinism seems to exists within classical logic especially in the classical Sequent Calculus LK (since Natural Deduction is inherently intuitionistic). Although there are a kind of connection between the two branches of the law of the excluded-middle (prove ~A then focus on A by retraction), to prove a statement of the shape (A or ~A) amount to try to prove A, then ~A, then return to A… (e.g P?=NP). We can’t predict the outcome (if there’s is one). We can also remark that classical logic (in its pure form) lack of computational content (non-confluency) but is the more natural way of reasoning for humans (even though it was rejected by intuitionism). Does all of that have any connection at all with the separation between humans and machines?

  10. How does mathematical concepts emerges from thoughts? How does intuitive concepts such as sets, numbers and functions emerges? Numbers for instance, have several definitions (Church, Parigot, Peano, Von Neumann, Infinite decimals/binary/n-ary or even composition of morphisms in monoids as unitary categories…). Is there any dialogue within our own mind (subject/subject) or with our environment (subject/object) ensuring this emergence? Is there any (well justified) classes of possible conceptions derived by interactions (either subject/subject or subject/object) ? For instance, plurality, unity, existence, universality… Is there a correlation with our conception of space and time? (are numbers emerging from our conception of time and sets from our conception of space?)

  11. What is the point of convergence of the dialectical evolution between humans and machines? The point zero is the point where humans first thought about automatizing their activities (before computers). Let’s call these automatizing mechanisms “machines”. Humans made machines to improve their life and freeing them from repetitive behaviors. The human life which could be defined over a single activity thus evolved by changing its activities. The more machines are improved, the more the concept of human is redefined. During the era of Artificial Intelligence, machines could approximate human intelligence but then redefined the meaning of intelligence itself in the process. The creation of challenges for machines implicitly lead to the improvement of humanity (always a step ahead of machines). Will the role of human and machines merge more and more through the time? Where does that dialectical evolution lead? Does it create conflicts with social and psychological aspects of humanity?

  12. What is the ontological status of the Internet? The Internet has a quite simple purpose: we can use it to communicate with other people in the world. It seems already fabulous but unexpectedly we may have created something with a very deep and precious meaning. What is the meaning of the Internet? What does it compute? Thanks to the massive storage of data, the Internet seems to include a record of humanity constantly reflecting its current state. More than that, the Internet seems to globally describe humanity and its knowledge. Is it a kind of mirror of a brain of the human kind as a whole? It may be only partially true for the moment (considering the current status of the Internet).
