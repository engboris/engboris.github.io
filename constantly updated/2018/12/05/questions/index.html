Questions

December 5, 2018
constantly updated

Here are some questions I’m interested in. These questions may have no meaning or true content (Sometimes I’m not even sure myself about the meaning of all that). Please tell me if there’re any existing related works. I will update this list to match my current understanding.

  • How does logic and computation manipulate and is affected by space and time? Linear Logic seems to take logic from mental references to the primitive intuition of space and time. Mathematical proofs which were seen as sequential mental acts evolving through time became proof-nets describing topological manipulations operated by logic. The lost of time is recovered in the works on polarization and focalization, exhibiting a mechanism of interaction by a dialogue between positives and negatives. How does interaction and information evolve in space and time? What can Logic teach us about the study of time (sequentiality and concurrency)? What can the study of space (graph theory, topology) teach us about the mechanisms of logic?

  • Are reductionist approaches to logic limited? Should we prefer a holistic one? Is exploring the structure of Logic and Computation enough to understand them? Category Theory capture quite well logical and computational phenomenon but one can wonder if something does escape the categorical view. What are the limits of the categorical view? It seems that Category Theory makes some assumptions reflecting how we understand abstract concepts. Categorical entities are high-level and considered up-to-isomorphism thus forgetting locativity and altering identification (existing in computer’s programs and architectures). While the categorical interpretation of logic identify proofs with the same dynamics (w.r.t cut-elimination) the geometry of interaction relate them with a procedure of execution. Does the categorical view format (positively? negatively?) our understanding?

  • Does the pragmatic approach of computation solve the identity crisis of Logic? It was once thought that classical logic didn’t have any computational interpretation. Then came a quite surprising interpretation given by the logical interpretation of innocent instructions (such as call/cc from Scheme). Can everyday programming provide a significant creativity to Logic? What is the logical interpretation of usual entities of practical programming? Can the practical use of computer science and programming (which is pragmatic) change our understanding of Logic (always influenced by paths of ideas and our understanding of concepts)? It may be interesting to investigate the computational meaning of some great mathematical axioms/theorems.

  • How are Logic and Computation related to Cognition and Nature? Linear Logic is used in type systems for quantum functional programming languages but also to describe higher-order quantum circuits through proof nets. How is it connected to quantum computation and quantum information led by the laws of quantum mechanics? Is there really any connection at all? Is it an exaggeration to relate cognition, computation, nature, quantum, and logic? Logic and computation seem to be part of the nature but the nature itself doesn’t seem to truly compute or reason in a discrete framework (or maybe even not at all).

  • What is the origin of historical prejudices and misconceptions of Logic? What does the history of Logic teach us about the way humans think and what/why do they believe? Like Physics and Biology, should Logic be taken as a science (observing interaction as a natural phenomenon)? It may be interesting to investigate the biological, psychological and sociological aspects of Logic. Why was Logic thought and organized the way it was? How can the understanding of our failure prevent us from going in wrong directions?

  • What makes something difficult or easy? Implicit Computation Complexity allows to express computational complexity classes by syntactical restrictions on formal languages. What makes the complexity of a class seems now to have a shape. It is then interesting to explore the deep structure of these restrictions. It seems that algebraic tools may give an understanding we didn’t have with the algorithmic view. The recent Geometric Complexity Theory seems to provide satisfying answers based on algebraic and geometrical arguments. Moreover, complexity seems to be associated with the formulation of exponentials in Linear Logic and an associated choice of algebra.

  • What Logic and Computation really are and how are they similar of different? Even today it is not clear what computation and logic are even though we thought we understood several times. The Curry-Howard isomorphism tells us that logic and computation share a common structure and are deeply related. Martin-Löf’s Type Theory and proof assistants exploit the idea by unifying proofs and formulas with programs and types in the same framework. Taking the idea to the extreme, is Logic a mere alias of Convergent Computation? It seems that the answer is no. Something similar to what separate analytic and synthetic seem to deeply separate computation and what we call logic. Logic has both a restrictive and normative nature.
