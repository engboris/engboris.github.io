On Intuitionism and Classicism

December 7, 2018
logicsemi-technical

Introduction

Is there several logics? A unique and right logic? Intuitionism was introduced during the so-called foundational crisis of mathematics in the beginning of the 20th century by mathematicians such as H. Poincaré. Strongly defended by L.E.J Brouwer [1], it constitutes both a critic of the mathematical practice and an opposition to formalism in favor of intuition and construction. I provide an understanding of intuitionism and classicism to the ones not familiar with it (but already know it) especially aimed at students in Computer Science. We start from the question of the reliability of logic and mathematics.

The location of certainty

Practice shows that mathematics has a great reliability but in the time of Brouwer, the appearence of non-euclidean geometries resulted in the emergence of doubts regarding this reliability. What right allows us to put a proposition as axiom or to use a particular rule of deduction? Where does the certainty of mathematics lies? Two school of philosophy tried to answer that last question:

  • Formalism (German): it is located in the paper, that is in the symbols we write, in the linguistics of mathematics, which are concrete, elementary and combinatorial
  • Intuitionism (French): it is located in the human’s intuition, that is, we should rely to what is accessible to our intuition, to what we truly know, what we can show/exhibit

Something still links both of them: the rejection of the correlation between mathematical laws and the laws of nature. In other words they’re both strongly subjectivist conceptions of logic and mathematics. We focus on intuitionism and its viewpoint regarding logic.

Subjectivism and constructivism

Science rely on empirical entities thus defining certainty on observation and experience. It may be a good start to find the certainty of logic. From the viewpoint of intuitionism, certainty appears when we can make the existence of mathematical entities explicit (opposing semantic realism and its external existence of entities). It has several consequences:

  • A revision of infinity. Brouwer rejected actual infinity (e.g ) but admitted potential infinity (e.g the inductive definition of ).
  • The rejection of the law of excluded-middle because it allows us to avoid to prove either or .
  • The rejection of proof by contradiction (i.e ) for the same reason.
  • A proof of existence must provide explicitly an entity and a proof of

The rejection of logical laws was quite shocking since they were both strongly used in the mathematical practice. Intuitionism is a subjectivist ideology because it only accept what we, humans, know, understand and can construct against any theory of truth and reality coming from God associating either or to propositions. We have to accept that we don’t necessarily know and have to consider the undecidability of propositions. Brouwer defended pure intuition instead of a linguistical and mechanical thus belittling view of mathematics so he was against any formalism in logic. Ironically, himself and one of his students, A. Heyting (and independently A. Kolmogorov) are behind the formalization of constructivism through the BHK interpretation. Logic is now divided in two: classical logic and intuitionistic logic.

Now, let’s explore the technical content of intuitionism.

Technical interpretation

No, intuitionism isn’t weaker. It can even be seen as stronger because intuitionistic logic is a logic where only constructions emerges. Not only it has a power that classical logic don’t have but moreover, the conception of classical truth isn’t lost.

Model-theoretic interpretation

As I understand it, classical logic was interpreted naturally with truth values either or associated to a logical proposition. As the concept of provability changed in the intuitionistic framework, we have to switch to Heyting models or Kripke models. It seems to me that they are rather ad-hoc constructions which are used because they work and ignoring the deep mechanism of intuitionism.

In Heyting models we usually interpret propositions in either a presentation of truth, falsity or “intermediate truth values” (e.g ordered by inclusion). These values represent the absence of knowledge “I don’t know” that we can assign to generally undecidable propositions such as the law of excluded middle. In Kripke models we do something similar by reifying a “world of all possibilities” allowing us to model the unknown and the unknowable. Therefore, the truth value of is “I don’t know”. Is that satisfyfing?

Proof-theoretic interpretation

The proof-theoretical interpretation is very interesting. Let’s explore it in the Natural Deduction and the Sequent Calculus .

  • The system is inherently and naturally intuitionistic because it simulates reasoning with implicit structural rules on the context and with determinism in the unique conclusion. Any reference to sub-formula in the final statement already appears proved in the top of the derivation tree. We can make it classical by adding a classical law but it’s indeed an artificial extension.

  • The sequent calculus is especially interesting because we can make it either classical or intuitionistic depending of if we restrict the right-hand part of the sequent to at most one formula or an arbitrary number of formulas. This peculiarity is not innocent and purely technical, the right-space restriction allows to focus only on a unique conclusion while laws such as the excluded-middle allows to diverge by kind of “jumps” in the reasoning. Having the focus only on the construction of the conclusion enforces constructivity.

Computational interpretation

The system being isomorphic to the -calculus through the Curry-Howard isomorphism, the computational interpretation is straightforward:

  • Intuitionistic proofs are typed functional programs .
  • Lambda terms can also be seen as a kind of logs or annotation describing the structure of the proof.

In particular, intuitionism captures computational decidability. What is provable is what is computable by a sequential functional program. We have a correspondence between computationally decidable and logically decidable in this framework. As in the computation only operates on a unique output.

Conclusion: human mind and non-determinism

To conclude, right-hand restriction on space forbids contraction/infinity on the conclusion but enforce constructivity and sequentiality. We may think that some classical truths are lost in this new intuitionistic framework but an encoding with double-negation allows to simulate classical proofs in a sequential way by a mechanism of jump/pointer/saving on the left-hand side which acts as a kind of memory.

But still, classical logic remains the most natural way of reasoning for humans. Intuitionism fits the point of view of sequential machines but classicism apparently don’t. What makes $\vdash A \lor \lnot A$ provable? It seems to be the human ability of retraction (which is destructive for sequential machines). The excluded-middle is indeed provable if we choose to prove $\lnot A$, then introduce $A$ in the context and change our mind to prove $A$ instead (as suggested by P. Wadler’s devil):

Then the right-hand part $LK$’s sequents should be seen as a space of non-determinism where we can postpone partial proofs to later. Only one part is proven but we don’t know which one. This induces that $\Gamma \vdash B_1, …, B_n$ may be seen as the superposition of sequential programs $\Gamma \vdash_i B_i$ in parallel for all $i$ also giving to the $⅋$ of Linear Logic a role of parallelization.

This leads to a reflection on the status of parallelism and non-determinism in Logic and Computation. D. Knuth once suggested that consciousness was sequential and unconsciousness parallel but that’s another question.
